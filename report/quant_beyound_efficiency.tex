\begin{abstract}
	
Quantization is a widely adopted technique that allows efficient inference on language models. A great deal of research has been conducted to understand
the gap between full and quantized models, including utilitarian and alignment aspects~\cite{liu2025quantizationhurtsreasoningempirical,kharinaev,marcuzzi,rath,jin,kirsten}, 
but a more comprehensive view that combines passive benchmark evaluations with active adversarial attacks is still lacking.
This work audits the impact of quantization on domains including model safeguards, instruction following, ethics and bias.
Through benchmarks, we discovered that model safeguards, bias and deception can degrade under quantization,
 while other capabilities like truthfulness, reasoning remain more stable in Qwen3 4B.
Furthermore, we verify the degradation of model safeguards by quantifying the increase in compliance with unsafe and deceptive requests and
 by demonstrating universal adversarial suffix attacks that are benign for full model but jailbreak quantized models. 
The findings indicate that it is risky to assume that the performance and 
robustness can transfer evenly through different capability domains from full models to quantized models.

\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
LLMs are increasingly being used in real-world, safety-critical environments, 
and quantization methods are necessary to deploy these models on hardware with limited resources.
Recent works have shown that quantization can diminish the performance of specific models in
certain capability domains, including reasoning \cite{liu2025quantizationhurtsreasoningempirical}, bias \cite{kirsten} and model safeguards \cite{kharinaev}.
While papers often evaluate models in passive and active forms of evaluation separately, we find a need for an approach that 
complements passive benchmark evaluations with active attacks, to provide stronger evidence for model decay under quantization. 

We address the following research questions:  
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Do model capabilities transfer in a quantized model consistently across different domains especially safeguards, bias and deception?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} What factors dictate whether a benchmark remains robust or exhibits degradation under quantization?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:} Does quantization increase an LLM's vulnerability to attacks regarding its safeguards?

\end{itemize}
We investigate these effects passively using multiple-choice and free-generation benchmarks,
and actively by deploying a quantization-aware adversarial suffix attack.
Through passive evaluations, we establish a wide view of the LLM's behavior under quantization.
Concretely, we evaluate the model across several quantization configurations (AWQ, INT4, and INT8),
comparing them to the BF16 baseline across domains such as reasoning, code generation, 
and model safeguards. Finally, as our benchmark evaluations demonstrate that model safeguards are particularly
 susceptible to quantization-induced decay, we perform an in-depth evaluation of the model's safety degradation.
Concretely, we show that model safeguards can be actively exploited by demonstrating an adversarial suffix attack that
is benign on the BF16 baseline but successfully triggers jailbreaks on the INT4 quantized version. 
 \par

Our key findings are the following:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Not all model capabilities transfer evenly with quantization. In particular, we observe that performance decreases in critical safety domains such as instruction following, safety refusal, and deception.
	\item We find factors for benchmark degradation such as the evaluation method and model coherence decay. 
	\item While multiple-choice benchmarks with log-likelihood ranking stay stable, free-generation capabilities can significantly decay, as seen in the Qwen-3 1.7B model under INT4.
	\item We identify adversarial suffix attacks that are benign for BF16 models, but successfully jailbreak the INT4 model. 
\end{itemize}

In conclusion, our findings suggest that models cannot be assumed to have the same safeguards or capabilities when quantized, 
which means that model safeguards and capabilities must be actively audited post-quantization before deployment, especially in safety-critical domains.

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Quantization}
Rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have led to a steady growth in model sizes, with modern models having hundreds of billions of parameters. Deploying these models requires significant amounts of video RAM (VRAM) creating a major bottleneck. One technique to mitigate this issue is quantization. This maps higher precision representations (such as brain floating point 16) of the model parameters to lower formats (such as 8-bit integers). This sharply reduces the memory footprint, but can also introduce a loss of model accuracy.
Widely-adopted strategies include Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) and Post-Training Quantization (PTQ). QAT introduces a fine-tuning step on a representative dataset to allow the model to adapt to lower precision formats at the cost of additional computation. In contrast, PTQ is applied directly on a trained model without further fine-tuning steps.
Quantization can target the model parameters (Weight Quantization) or additionally at the inference state (Activation Quantization). In this work, we concentrate on PTQ and Weight Quantization as they are the most widely adopted strategies in practice through their computational low costs. \subsection{Benchmarking}
To evaluate the performance of LLMs and ensure comparability between different models, a broad range of standardized benchmarks has been established. 
These benchmarks assess their performance across a diverse range of domains, including mathematical reasoning, general knowledge, bias and robustness against jailbreak attempts. This is a non-trivial evaluation task, as it is not clear if the benchmark answers were contained in the training data.
Benchmarks do not only differ in the domain they assess, but also in their evaluation protocol.
In multiple choice and log-likelihood ranking the model is presented with predefined answer options. Performance is evaluated either by calculating the log-likelihood of each answer candidate and selecting the highest-probability option, or by parsing the generated response using regular expression.
In free-form generation the model generates a response during inference. This is evaluated by either exact-matching (e.g., extracting a numerical answer) or by utilizing a LLM-as-a-judge model to categorize the response.
Free generation allows the model to generate a natural response during inference. Another LLM is utilized as a judge to rate the answer as correct or false according to the task.
In assessing quantized model performance, the evaluation methodology is of crucial interest. While log-likelihood ranking only detects shifts in token probabilities, free-form generation reveals how these shifts accumulate over time, leading to a sharper degradation in real-world answer quality than in the log-likelihood setting.


\subsection{Related Works}

While widely adopted MC benchmarks often remain stable under quantization, free-form generation reveals severe safety alignment degradation, demonstrating the risk of masking real-world deployment safety~\cite{kharinaev}. Marcuzzi et al.~\cite{marcuzzi} further explored the reduction in safety alignment on quantization. They found a limited influence on metrics like toxicity and sentiment, while stereotypes and unfairness were amplified. Quantization appears to reverse the instruction tuning step instilled to align model responses with human preferences, forcing the model to fall back to underlying stereotypical associations captured in the pretraining phase~\cite{rath}. Even though quantized models can maintain high performance on knowledge-based tasks, bias assessment remains mixed. While Jin et al.~\cite{jin} found a decrease in bias after quantization, Kirsten et al.~\cite{kirsten} connected this effect to the model architecture family, finding bias spikes in certain families and small effects in others.

\hv{short jailbreak reference?}

Various ways of attacking language models have been studied. We in particular focus on the following two types of attacks. Adversarial suffix attacks try to find a prompt suffix that jailbreaks the model to induce misaligned generations. Zou et al.~\cite{GCG} reported the GCG method, which constructs universal adversarial suffixes that jailbreaks the open source Vicuna-7B models. The discovered attack suffixes even generalize to work on closed-source models including GPT-3.5. Later works~\cite{Faster-GCG,I-GCG} improve the efficiency of finding the adversarial suffix.
On the other hand, quantization-aware attacks refer to the utilization of difference between full and quantized models. Researchers from ETH Zürich~\cite{QCB} first reported the technique to train a model that is benign in full precision, but misaligned after quantization.

\section{Benchmarks}
\subsection{Datasets and metrics}

We chose a variety of benchmarks to evaluate model performance across different quantization levels across different domains. Our curation focused on balancing log-likelihood ranking (LLR) and free form generation (FFG), where fixed answers in the LLR setting limit potential model degradation, which is revealed under the FFG setting. This approach ensures we are able to capture both fundamental capability retention (LLR) and degradation (FFG).

The following benchmarks were chosen to represent these paradigms:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple-Choice (LLR) Benchmarks:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{GPQA}: Expert-level reasoning in biology, physics and chemistry.
        \item \textbf{MMLU}: Multitask performance across 57 different domains e.g. US history, law and more.
        \item \textbf{MMLU-Pro}: Extended variant of MMLU with more answer choices.
        \item \textbf{ARC}: 3rd to 9th grade science exams questions.
        \item \textbf{BBQ}: Measuring social bias and stereotypes across 9 categories.
        \item \textbf{TruthfulQA}: Assessment of the reproduction of common human misconceptions and rumors.
        \item \textbf{ETHICS}: Alignment to broad moral and ethical principles.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Free Generation Benchmarks:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{SORRY-Bench} \cite{sorry_bench_hf}: Measuring refusal of unsafe user requests across different phrasing. Focus on dangerous, illegal or inappropriate tasks. 
        \item \textbf{DeceptionBench} \cite{deceptionbench_hf}: Measures the model's willingness to follow prompts that have deceptive behaviour/goals.
        \item \textbf{InFoBench} \cite{infobench_hf}: Evaluating complex instruction following capabilities.
        \item \textbf{WildChat-nontoxic} \cite{wildchat_hf}: General utility under broad real-world user requests.
        \item \textbf{MGSMdirect}: Multilingual grade school math word problems in 11 different languages.
        \item \textbf{HumanEval}: Python code generation capabilities and functional correctness.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

LLR evaluations were executed utilizing the lm-evaluation-harness framework~\cite{eval-harness}. For the benchmarks SORRY-Bench, DeceptionBench, InFoBench and Wildchat-nontoxic we implemented custom evaluation pipelines with a deepseek-4-flash model acting as a judge.
All experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA A100 GPU. The maximum sequence length was capped at 4096 tokens. Generation limits followed each benchmark's standard lm-evaluation-harness configuration (e.g., 2048 tokens for MMLU-Pro, 1024 for BBH and HumanEval).
LLR benchmarks followed a zero-shot learning approach to prevent any bias influence from the training set.
In contrast, FFG benchmarks were evaluated under 5-shot context learning to ground the model's outputs.

\section{Quantization-Aware Adversarial Suffix Attack}

The matrix study on benchmarks shows the performance difference of full and quantized models in their usage of the intended scenario.
But when deployed in real-world applications, models are also exposed to malicious adversaries that try to mount attacks in unexpected ways.
Therefore, the passive evaluations of the models against benchmarks are not sufficient to guarantee their safety. There can still be vulnerabilities that adversaries can utilize. 
In particular, people tend to assume that the robustness of full models will transfer to the quantized ones, but it does not hold in general.
This section introduces our novel discovery of quantization-aware adversarial suffix attacks.

Our attack technique combines two concepts: adversarial suffix attacks and quantization-aware attacks.
Quantization-aware attacks sharply demonstrate the risk of the gap between full and quantized models.
The robustness of the full model does not necessarily transfer, and worse still, it can even be maliciously utilized to mount a supply-chain attack. On the other hand, the suffix attacks have a more practical adversary model: it works on commonly used open source models, and the suffixes are generic to a certain extent. 
One question arises naturally from the intersection of these two vulnerabilities: whether it is possible to construct quantization-aware adversarial suffix attacks, as is illustrated in~\Cref{fig:quant-aware-adv-suffix}.

\subsection{Attack Method}

The adversarial examples are constructed by optimizing the input with the objective of the attack.
For adversarial suffixes, the objective is to maximize the probability of \textit{getting an affirmative answer}.
In this section, we want to check whether there exist suffixes that are quantization-aware. Therefore the objective has two components: refusal on full model and affirmative answer on quantized model. This is formalized as follows.

\paragraph{Formalizing the adversarial objective}
We use $x$ to denote token sequences, representing the text to process and generate.
We consider a LLM $p$ as a probabilistic model $p(x_{n+1}\mid x_{1:n})$, which corresponds to the probability of generating the next token $x_{n+1}$ given the existing token sequence $x_{1:n}$. Then the probability to generate a sequence $x_{n+1:n+H}$ will be
\[
p(x_{n+1:n+H} | x_{1:n}) = \prod_{i=1}^{H}p(x_{n+i} | x_{1:n+i-1}).
\]
We use $\tilde{p}$ to denote the quantized model.
Since we expect the adversarial suffix to be universal, we consider a conceptual distribution $D$ of sensitive prompts and their answers.
We use $q$ to represent the prompt questions, $a_{+}$ to represent their affirmative answers, $a_{-}$ to represent their refusals, and $s$ to represent the unique adversarial suffix. 
Then the desired qunatization-aware adversarial suffix can be expressed as
\[
s^* = \operatorname*{arg\,max}_{s} \left [ \mathbb{E}_{q, a_{+}, a_{-} \sim D}\ (\tilde{p}(a_{+} | q \mathbin{++} s) + \lambda \cdot p(a_{-} | q \mathbin{++} s)) \right ].
\]
Here the intuition is to maximize the two probabilities of refusal on full model and fulfillment on quantized model, and the ratio is controlled by parameter $\lambda$.
Compared to the vanilla GCG algorithm~\cite{GCG} for adversarial examples, this objective introduces the refusal term on full model.

\subsection{Experiment Setup}

We need aggressive assumptions and simplifications to make $s^*$ computable.

The first simplification is log probability. We replace $p(x|y)$ with their log values. This simplification also allows us to use the model logit output as the log probability.
Also, we calculate the question-answer expectation by sampling from a benchmark.
Then we replace $p(a_{-} | q \mathbin{++} s)$ with $-p(a_{+} | q \mathbin{++} s)$. This means we assume that minimizing fulfillment is equivalent to maximizing refusal.

Adversarial examples for images can be calculated by gradient descent, because visual models are end-to-end differentiable from input to output.
This trick does not work for LLMs since the step from token IDs to embeddings is a discrete operation.
Following the practice of GCG~\cite{GCG}, we resolve the optimization by a heuristic greedy searching algorithm. The searching starts from the initial suffix (repeated `!'), and flips the token according to the gradient information on the one-hot vectors, one at a time. Intuitively, at every round the algorithm flips one token in the suffix that has largest impact in the objective.

During real inference, the input sequence after applying the chat template is shown in~\Cref{fig:prompt-structure}. The suffix is inserted after the user prompt, before the \texttt{assistant} tokens. The target is an affirmative answer prefix, and the training follows the LLR style. We conduct one round of forward calculation, and the gradient is calculated based on target logits. In the training we also adopt a learning curriculum. The training starts with one single prompt, then progressively expands to a set of \texttt{n\_train} prompts for generalization.

According to our previous results from benchmarking, the INT4 quantization behaviour deviates the most from the full model.
Our attack experiment investigates the same Qwen 3-4B model on the BF16 and INT4 precisions.

\paragraph{Evaluation} We evaluate the harmful compliance rate of the BF16 and INT4 models, with and without the discovered adversarial suffix. The compliance evaluation takes two criteria:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RE-Judge:} following the practice in GCG~\cite{GCG}, the judger checks whether the generation contains typical refusal expressions, such as ``I cannot'', ``I am sorry'', ``I must decline'', etc.
  \item \textbf{LM-Judge:} following the practice in SORRY-Bench, a language model is prompted to evaluate whether the generation is an essential fulfillment.
  Here we use a fine-tuned Mistral-7B-Instruct model as the judge.
\end{enumerate}

Intuitively, the RE-Judge tests syntactical refusal and the LM-Judge has a closer connection with semantics.

\subsection{Results Analysis}

We conduct the experiment on the Qwen-3 4B model, and use the base split of SORRY-Bench as the dataset of misaligned requests. 
The adversarial suffix attack maximizes the gap between refusal of BF16 full model and fulfillment of the INT4 quantized model.
We sample 24 prompts for training, and evaluate on the 426 held-out prompts for testing.
The initial adversarial suffix is 20 tokens in length and starts with repeated `!'.
We take the parameter $\lambda = 1$, and conducted the searching algorithm for 500 steps.
The experiment took 3 hours on a node with 4 $\times$ A100-40G GPUs.
The result is shown in~\Cref{tab:adv-suffix}.

Statistics show that with the suffix, the harmful compliance rate between BF16 and INT4 enlarges from around 8\% to $13\% \sim 35\%$ on the test set.
This confirms that the adversarial suffix does trigger jailbreaking selectively on the quantized model. 
Another interesting finding is that the compliance rate by the RE-Judge is much higher than the LM-Judge.
This indicates that the attack suffix often triggers answers that only comply in the tone but are not genuinely harmful, which is confirmed by individual case studies.
The difference is within our expectation because the objective is also constructed by the LLR syntax.

\section{Conclusion}

\textbf{RQ1: Do the capabilities of a quantized model transfer consistently across various domains?}

We have shown that models perform inconsistently on multiple-choice benchmarks when evaluated with LLR in Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_multi_choice_tf}. 
On the FFG experiment with the LM judge in Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_free_gen_lm_judge}, we observed that model safeguards,
 such as DeceptionBench~\cite{deceptionbench_hf} and SORRY-bench~\cite{sorry_bench_hf}, significantly decline while instruction following remains stable. 
We can conclude that model capabilities do not necessarily transfer equally across different domains.  

\textbf{RQ2: What factors dictate whether a benchmark remains robust or exhibits degradation under quantization?}

In our benchmarks in Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_free_gen_lm_judge}, we also found that safety and refusal tasks that are inherently vague or ambiguous are more affected by quantization 
than tasks with explicit boundaries that are easier to judge. Specifically, we observed that the more nuanced
behaviors assessed in DeceptionBench are more sensitive to performance decline compared to the more unambiguous safety violations evaluated in SORRY-bench.

The evaluation method dictates the observed performance stability under quantization, especially in the smaller 1.7B model. 
As shown by Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_1.7B_tf} and Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_1.7B_free_gen}, 
quantization affects free generation and log-likelihood ranking (LLR) benchmarks unequally. If the model falls below
 a certain size parameter and is quantized too aggressively, it loses its free-form generation capability, 
 yet it still evaluates validly under LLR benchmarks. In the example in Figure~\ref{fig:coherence_collapse_example} it's shown that,
 in free generation, the model can cascade down an incoherent generation path. This is completely prevented in the LLR setting, where the candidate 
answers are pre-defined and fixed.

Furthermore, another reason why LLR evaluates more consistently than FFG is because it does not require an LLM judge, regex parsing, or other deterministic evaluation methods to score.
This makes it less sensitive to judgment failures, parsing errors, or minor mistakes.

\textbf{RQ3: Does the decline in safeguards expose LLMs to unique vulnerabilities?}

Our benchmarks (Table~\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_free_gen_lm_judge}) show that models decline significantly in safeguards, and are more likely to generate deceptive answers and refuse unsafe requests less often.
To explore this further, we exploited vulnerabilities in the quantized models using an adversarial suffix attack. 
We successfully discovered adversarial suffixes that trigger more harmful compliance only on quantized models. It indicates that people cannot assume the transferability of attack robustness from the full model to the quantized one.




\section{Limitations}
The benchmark evaluations in \Cref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_free_gen_lm_judge} are judged via a language model. LLM-as-a-judge is an inconsistent measure of the model performance.
We evaluated only the Qwen3 models, we do not know if our results generalize to other model families.      

The quantization-aware attack by adversarial suffix still has many limitations. The model tends to produce superficially compliant text, rather than genuinely informative harmful text. Also, the attack suffix will degrade the generation quality in format. 
From the perspective of experiment design, we didn't investigate the other direction -- whether there are adversarial suffixes that jailbreak only the full model. Our conjecture is that such suffixes also exist, and the full model is not always safer than the quantized one.
From our preliminary experiments, there is no evidence supporting that the suffix discovered on Qwen generalizes to other open source models.
