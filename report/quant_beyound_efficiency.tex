\section{Introduction}
 LLMs are increasingly being used in real-world, safety-critical environments, and quantization methods are necessary to deploy LLMs on hardware with limited resources, as they reduce the memory footprint of LLMs.     
Recent studies have shown that quantization can reduce the performance of specific models in certain capability domains, including reasoning, coding and bias and trustworthiness. However, no study has yet taken a more comprehensive look at the degradation of different capability domains, with a primary focus on capabilities affecting model safeguards, including ethical judgement, deception, and safety refusal. This work aims to fill this gap. 

We address the following research questions:  
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Do model capabilities transfer in a quantized model consistently across different domains especially safeguards, bias and deception?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} Does quantization increase an LLMs vulnerability to attacks regarding their safeguards?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:} What factors dictate whether a benchmark remains robust or exhibits a steep decline under quantization?
\end{itemize}

To answer these questions, we conducted comprehensive evaluations of the Qwen-3 4B model under quantization.
We investigate these effects passively using multiple-choice and free-generation benchmarks, and actively by deploying a quantization-aware adversarial suffix attack.
We evaluate the model across several quantization configurations (AWQ, INT4 and INT8), comparing them to the full-precision BF16 baseline and we 
explore the feasibility of adversarial attacks that exploit vulnerabilities created by the degradation of the model's safeguards through quantization.
\par

Our key findings are the following:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Not all model capabilities transfer evenly with quantization. We observe that some domains are more affected by quantization than others.    
	\item The models performance decreases in critical safety domains such as instruction following, safety-refusal and deception when quantized.
    \item Multiple choice benchmarks evaluated with log likelihood tend to stay more stable in quantization compared to free generation benchmarks.
	\item Quantized models are more vulnerable to adversarial suffix jailbreaking attacks due to the decline of their safety mechanisms. 
\end{itemize}
In conclusion, our findings suggest that models cannot be assumed to have the same safeguards under stronger quantization levels. 
Which means that model safeguards must be actively audited post-quantization before deployment, especially in safety-critical domains.



\section{Background and Related Work}

[Introduction Quantization]
Rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have lead to a steady growth in model sizes, with modern models having hundreds of billions of parameters. Deploying these models requires significant amounts of video RAM (VRAM) creating a major bottleneck. One technique to mitigate this issue is quantization. This maps higher precision representations (such as brain floating point 16) of the model parameters to lower formats (such as 8-bit integers). This drasticly reduces the memory footprint, but can also introduce a loss of model accuracy.
Widely-adopted strategies include Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) and Post-Training Quantization (PTQ). QAT introduces a fine-tuning step on a representative dataset to allow the model to adapt to lower precision formats at the cost of additional computation. In contrast, PTQ is applied directly on a trained model without further fine-tuning steps. 
Quantization can target the model parameters (Weight Quantization) or additionaly at the inference states (Activation Quantization). In this work, we concentrate on PTQ and Weight Quantization as they are the most widely adopted strategies in practice through their computational low costs. \section{Quantization-Aware Adversarial Suffix Attack}

The matrix study on benchmarks shows the performance difference of full and quantized models in their usage of the intended scenario.
But when deployed in real-world applications, models are also exposed to malicious adversaries that try mount attacks in unexpected ways.
Therefore, the static evaluations of the models against benchmarks are not sufficient to guarantee their safety. There can still be vulnerabilities that adversaries can utilize. 

During the deployment of quantized models, the vulnerabilities against attacks are easily overlooked.
People tend to assume that the robustness of full models will transfer to the quantized ones, but it does not hold in general.
This section introduces our novel discovery of quantization-aware adversarial suffix attacks.

This technique combines two concepts: adversarial suffix attacks and quantization-aware attacks.
Quantization-aware attacks sharply demonstrate the risk of the gap between full and quantized models.
The robustness of full-model does not necessary transfer, and worse still, it can even be maliciously utilized to mount a supply-chain attack. On the other hand, the suffix attacks have a more practical adversary model: it works on commonly used open source models, and the suffixes are generic to a certain extent. 
One question rises naturally from the intersection of these two vulnerabilities: whether it is possible to construct quantization-aware adversarial suffix attacks, as is illustrated in~\Cref{fig:quant-aware-adv-suffix}.

\subsection{Attack Method}

The adversarial examples are constructed by optimizing the input with the objective of the attack.
For adversarial suffixes, the objective is to maximize the probability of \textit{getting an affirmative answer}.
In this section, we want to check whether there exist suffixes that are quantization-aware. Therefore the objective has two components: refusal on full model and affirmative answer on quantized model. This is formalized as follows.

\paragraph{Formalizing the adversarial objective}
We use $x$ to denote token sequences, representing the text to process and generate.
We consider a LLM $p$ as a probabilistic model $p(x_{n+1}\mid x_{1:n})$, which corresponds to the probability of generating the next token $x_{n+1}$ given the existing token sequence $x_{1:n}$. Then the probability to generate a sequence $x_{n+1:n+H}$ will be
\[
p(x_{n+1:n+H} | x_{1:n}) = \prod_{i=1}^{H}p(x_{n+i} | x_{1:n+i-1}).
\]
We use $\tilde{p}$ to denote the quantized model.
Since we expect the adversarial suffix to be universal, we consider a conceptual distribution $D$ of sensitive prompts and their answers.
We use $q$ to represent the prompt questions, $a_{+}$ to represent their affirmative answers, $a_{-}$ to represent their refusals, and $s$ to represent the unique adversarial suffix. 
Then the desired qunatization-aware adversarial suffix can be expressed as
\[
s^* = \operatorname*{arg\,max}_{s} \left [ \mathbb{E}_{q, a_{+}, a_{-} \sim D}\ (\tilde{p}(a_{+} | q \mathbin{++} s) + \lambda \cdot p(a_{-} | q \mathbin{++} s)) \right ].
\]
Here the intuition is to maximize the two probabilities of refusal on full model and fulfillment on quantized model, and the ratio is controlled by parameter $\lambda$.
Compared to the vanilla GCG algorithm~\cite{GCG} for adversarial examples, this objective introduces the refusal term on full model.

\subsection{Experiment Setup}

We need aggresive assumptions and simplifications to make $s^*$ computable. 

The first simplification is log probability. We replace $p(x|y)$ with the their log values. This simplification also allows us to use the model logit output as the log probability.
Also, we calculate the question-answer expectation by sampling from a benchmark.
Then we replace $p(a_{-} | q \mathbin{++} s)$ with $-p(a_{+} | q \mathbin{++} s)$. This means we assume that minimizing fulfillment is equivalent to maximizing refusal.

Adversarial examples for images can be calculated by gradient decent, because visual models are end-to-end differentiable from input to output. 
This trick does not work for LLMs since the step from token IDs to embeddings is a discrete operation.
Following the practice of GCG~\cite{GCG}, we resolve the optimization by a heuristic greedy searching algorithm. The searching starts from the initial suffix (repeated `!'), and flips the token according to the gradient information on the one-hot vectors, one at a time. Intuitively, at every round the algorithm flips one token in the suffix that has largest impact in the objective.

During real inference, the input sequence after applying the chat template is shown in~\Cref{fig:prompt-structure}. The suffix is inserted after the user prompt, before the \texttt{assistant} tokens. The target is an affirmative answer prefix, and the training follows the teacher forcing style. We conduct one round of forward calculation, and the gradient is calculated based on target logits. In the training we also adopt a learning curriculum. The training starts with one single prompt, then progressively expands to a set of \texttt{n\_train} prompts for generalization.

\paragraph{Evaluation} We evaluate the harmful compliance rate of the BF16 and INT4 models, with and without the discovered adversarial suffix. The compliance evaluation takes two criteria:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RE-Judge:} following the practice in GCG~\cite{GCG}, the judger checks whether the generation contains typical refusal expressions, such as ``I cannot'', ``I am sorry'', ``I must decline'', etc.
  \item \textbf{LM-Judge:} following the practice in SORRY-Bench, a language model is prompted to evaluate whether the generation is an essential fulfillment.
  Here we use a fine-tuned Mistral-7B-Instruct model as the judge.
\end{enumerate}

Intuitively, the RE-Judge tests syntactical refusal and the LM-Judge has a closer connection with semantics.

\subsection{Results Analysis}

We conduct the experiment on the Qwen-3 4B model, and use the base split of SORRY-Bench as the dataset of misaligned requests. 
The adversarial suffix attack maximizes the gap between refusal of BF16 full model and fulfillment of the INT4 quantized model.
We sample 24 prompts for training, and evaluate on the 426 held-out prompts for testing.
The initial adversarial suffix is 20 token in length and starts with repeated `!'.
We take the parameter $\lambda = 1$, and conducted the searching algorithm for 500 steps.
The experiment tooks 3 hours on a node with 4 $\times$ A100-40G GPUs.
The result is shown in~\Cref{tab:adv-suffix}.

Statistics show that with the suffix, the harmful compliance rate between BF16 and INT4 enlarges from around 8\% to $13\% \sim 35\%$ on the test set.
This confirms that the adversarial suffix does trigger jailbreaking selectively on the quantized model. 
Another interesting finding is that the compliance rate by the RE-Judge is much higher than the LM-Judge.
This indicates taht the attack suffix often triggers answers that only comply in the tone but are not genuinely harmful, which is confirmed by individual case studies.
The difference is within our expectation because the objective is also constructed by the teacher forcing syntax.

\section{Conclusion}

RQ1: Do the capabilities of a quantized model transfer consistently across various domains?

Our evaluations have shown that models perform inconsistently on multiple-choice benchmarks when evaluated with teacher forcing. 
We find that benchmarks that measure model safeguards, such as DeceptionBench and SorryBench significantly decline.
This suggests that the model can successfully fulfill dangerous user instructions.
Furthermore, we can conclude that model capabilities do not necessarily transfer equally which implies that model capabilities are disjoint.   

RQ2: Does the decline in safeguards expose LLMs to unique vulnerabilities?

As our evaluations show, models decline significantly in safeguards, and are more likely to generate deceptive answers and refuse unsafe requests less often.
To explore this further, we exploited vulnerabilities in the quantized models using a quantization-aware adversarial suffix attack. 

RQ3: Why do some benchmarks remain consistent with quantization while others decline?
\ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_1.7B_free_gen} and \ref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_1.7B_tf} shows that quantization effects free generation and teacher forcing benchmarks unequally.
If the model falls under a certain size, and is quantized too strongly, it loses its free generation ability, but still evaluates
validly under teacher forcing benchmarks. 



\section{Limitations}
The benchmark evaluations in \Cref{tab:comparison_table_Qwen3_4B_free_gen_lm_judge} are judged via a language model. LLM-as-a-judge is an inconsistent measure of the model performance.
We evaluated only the Qwen3 models, we do not know if our results generalize to other model families.      

The quantization-aware attack by adversarial suffix still has many limitations. The model tends to produce superficially compliant text, rather than geniunely informative harmful text. Also, the attack suffix will degrade the generation quality in format. Our preliminary experiemnts show that the suffix discovered on Qwen does not generate to other open source models.
